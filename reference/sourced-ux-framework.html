01User Psychology Foundation

Who Are We Designing For?

🏗
Marcus, GC Project Manager
GENERAL CONTRACTOR · 12 YEARS EXP

Manages 3–6 concurrent jobs. Spends 40% of time on the phone chasing material quotes. Trusts gut-feel pricing. Hates software that "doesn't get construction."

Entry Emotion: Skeptical
Fear: Wasting time on another tool
Trigger: "How much cheaper can I get this?"
📦
Lisa, Supply Rep
REGIONAL SUPPLIER · ELECTRICAL MATERIALS

Manages B2B accounts. Relies on cold calls and trade shows. Sees unpredictable revenue. Knows she's missing sales she doesn't even know exist.

Entry Emotion: Curious but cautious
Fear: Low-quality leads, race to bottom pricing
Trigger: "Steady, qualified demand"
🔧
Danny, Small Sub
SUBCONTRACTOR · PLUMBING · 3-PERSON CREW

Buys 1–2x/month. Overpays without knowing it. Hates "enterprise" software. Needs something he can use between jobs on his phone.

Entry Emotion: Overwhelmed
Fear: Complexity, contracts, being locked in
Trigger: "I saved $400 on this one order"

Top 3 User Intents (What They WANT)

01 · CERTAINTY

Not features — certainty that they'll pay a fair price and get the materials on time. Remove the anxiety of "am I getting ripped off?"

02 · TIME BACK

They don't want procurement software. They want the 4 hours per week they spend chasing quotes back. That's what the platform sells.

03 · ZERO SURPRISES

Price surprises tank jobs. They want confirmed pricing before they commit, with delivery windows they can plan around. No "we'll confirm later."

Biggest Fears & Motivation Triggers

FEARS

  • Posting a requirement and getting zero responses
  • A supplier seeing their pricing data (competitors)
  • Committing to a platform that adds work, not removes it
  • Losing control of vendor relationships they built over years
  • Getting locked into a subscription that doesn't deliver

MOTIVATION TRIGGERS

  • Seeing a real dollar amount saved on first order
  • Getting a competitive bid within hours, not days
  • AI doing the work they hate (follow-ups, comparisons)
  • Peers using it ("my buddy's crew uses Sourced")
  • Specific proof: "avg 14% below market pricing"
02Full User Journey — End to End

Entry to Retention Loop

E
Entry — Discovery & Landing
🔴 Skeptical · Distracted · Time-starved

User arrives via word-of-mouth, Google, or LinkedIn. They have 90 seconds of patience. The landing page must answer: "What is this, who is it for, can I trust it?"

Intent

Evaluate in 60s if this is worth their time

Emotional State

Skeptical. They've seen "AI for construction" before.

Must Happen

Instant role recognition + a concrete, credible value claim

F
First Impression — Sign Up Flow
🟡 Curious · Cautiously Optimistic

If they clicked "Get Started," they're interested. This stage must not lose them. 3-field max signup. No credit card. Role selection routes the experience.

Intent

Get inside without friction or commitment anxiety

Emotional State

Optimistic but watching for red flags (too many fields, credit card)

Must Happen

Sub-60s signup. Immediate role-specific dashboard, not a generic home

A
Activation — First Value Action
🟡 Eager · Slightly Nervous

For contractors: post their first requirement in under 2 minutes. For suppliers: confirm their product catalog in under 5 minutes. Activation = first action that creates value, not just account setup.

Intent

See the platform actually work, not a demo

Emotional State

Eager to be impressed, ready to bounce if it's confusing

Must Happen

A requirement posted OR a bid received within the session. Zeigarnik loop opened.

C
Core Use — AI Procurement Cycle
🟢 Trusting · Waiting · Evaluating

AI agents collect bids, compare suppliers, surface ranked options. The user's job becomes review-and-decide, not gather-and-compare. This must feel magic: they posted a requirement and got 4 ranked bids without lifting a finger.

Intent

Make a confident procurement decision, fast

Emotional State

Impressed if it works. Anxious if it's slow or opaque.

Must Happen

Bid comparison view must be scannable in 30 seconds. One clear "Best Value" recommendation.

V
Value Realization — First Accepted Bid
🟢 Satisfied · Converted

The moment a bid is accepted and confirmation lands. This is the emotional peak of the journey. The platform must celebrate it: show how much they saved vs. market rate, send a confirmation, open the path to the next requirement.

Intent

Confirm the deal is real and reliable

Emotional State

Relieved. Impressed. Likely to tell a colleague.

Must Happen

Savings summary shown. Referral prompt appears. Next requirement CTA visible.

R
Retention Loop — Habitual Use
🟢 Habitual · Dependent

Sourced becomes the default behavior before any significant material purchase. The retention loop: new project → open Sourced → post requirement → receive bids → accept → track delivery. Monthly savings reports reinforce the habit.

Intent

Sourced is now the first step, not an afterthought

Emotional State

Reliant. Would feel the loss if the platform disappeared.

Must Happen

Push notifications for bid updates. Monthly savings report. Streak or usage momentum.

How UI Reduces Cognitive Load

  • One primary action per screen. Secondary actions have lower visual weight. Ghost links for destructive/exit actions.
  • Smart defaults everywhere. Delivery address pre-filled. Quantity defaults to previous order. Date defaults to "7 days from today."
  • Inline validation — never delay error feedback to form submit.
  • Collapsible optional fields. Core task completion requires the fewest fields possible.
  • Color used semantically, not decoratively. A red badge always means "action needed." Green always means "good."
  • Maximum 2 typefaces. Maximum 3 type sizes per screen. Hierarchy through weight and color, not proliferation.
  • All currency and percentage calculations done by the system. Users compare, not calculate.

09Psychology Mapping

Every Principle, Specifically Applied

HICK'S LAW
More choices = longer decision time. Reduce options to reduce paralysis.
Applied: Bid comparison shows max 4 bids above the fold. Role selection is binary (Contractor / Supplier). The requirement form has 4 required fields, not 12. Every screen has ONE primary action.
FOGG BEHAVIOR MODEL
Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt. All three must align.
Applied: Motivation: "$347 saved" on confirmation screen. Ability: Smart defaults, autocomplete, 4-field forms. Prompt: Push notification "Your bids are ready" fires at the exact moment of maximum relevance.
LOSS AVERSION
Losing $10 feels worse than gaining $10. Frame as avoiding loss.
Applied: "This bid expires in 18h." "You're currently paying 14% above market." Monthly savings report shows "You saved $1,240 — here's what you would have paid without Sourced." Churn prevention: "Cancel and lose your bid history and $X in pending savings."
ZEIGARNIK EFFECT
Incomplete tasks occupy more mental bandwidth than completed ones.
Applied: Progress bar on requirement form is never at 0% — starts at 30% (role selection already "done"). Dashboard shows requirements "In Progress" at the top. Incomplete onboarding shows a persistent "Finish setup to unlock bidding" prompt.
DEFAULT BIAS
People tend to accept pre-selected options without changing them.
Applied: AI-recommended bid is pre-selected (green border, "Best Value" badge). Delivery address defaults to project site. Quantity defaults to previous order amount. Notification preferences default to ON. The defaults are designed to drive the best outcome, not random.
SOCIAL PROOF
People follow the actions of others, especially peers.
Applied: Landing page: real contractor quotes with company names (with permission). Bid comparison: "47 contractors in RI have ordered from this supplier." Order confirmation referral prompt: "Share Sourced with a GC you trust." Supplier bid screen: win rate displayed ("You've won 4 of your last 6 bids").
10Adaptive UX Layer

The UI That Learns

ONBOARDING OVERLAY

Contextual tooltips on first-session dashboard. Not a tour — just single-step hints that appear when the user's cursor/focus approaches a key element for the first time. Dismissed on interaction, never on timer.

GUIDED FIRST REQUIREMENT

The requirement form on first use has an extra "Why we ask this" inline explanation next to each field. After first requirement is posted, these explanations disappear forever. Never condescending on repeat use.

SIMPLIFIED BID VIEW

First bid comparison screen hides advanced filters. Shows only: Price, Delivery Date, Rating. Advanced filtering appears from Session 3 onward as a secondary option — not removed, just de-emphasized.

EMPTY STATE COACHING

Every empty state on first use has a direct action prompt and a "Here's what this looks like with data" preview illustration. Never just a blank page with a message.

SMART DASHBOARD

Dashboard prioritizes what needs action: bids awaiting acceptance at top, followed by in-progress requirements, then completed orders. Returning users see action-oriented layout, not onboarding prompts.

QUICK REORDER

"Reorder this" appears on all past orders after Session 2. One-tap pre-fills the requirement form with last order's details. Returning users hate repeating themselves — this eliminates it.

REMEMBERED PREFERENCES

Preferred suppliers bookmarked after 2+ orders with the same supplier. "You've ordered from East Bay Supply 3 times — add to preferred suppliers?" Smart prompt, not automatic enrollment.

NOTIFICATION TUNING

After Month 1, a notification preferences screen is surfaced: "You're getting X notifications/week — customize what matters most." Reduces churn from notification fatigue.

BULK REQUIREMENTS

Power users unlock CSV upload for multi-line requirements. "Post 12 requirements at once" — shown only after 5+ individual requirements have been posted. Revealed through progressive disclosure, not upfront complexity.

ADVANCED FILTERS

Bid comparison unlocks full filtering after 10+ bid comparisons: filter by certified minority supplier, ISO rating, specific delivery time windows, payment terms. Appears as a "Filters" button that was previously hidden.

API ACCESS

After $5K+ in procurement volume, a "Connect your ERP" CTA appears. Surfaced as a logical next step for high-volume users — not as an upsell on a pricing page. Contextual, not commercial.

SAVINGS ANALYTICS

Full analytics dashboard with YTD savings, by-category breakdown, most-used suppliers, market benchmark comparison chart. Available from Month 3. Power users get the data they need to justify Sourced to their CFO.

Deliver this as a narrated screen walkthrough. Each block = one screen transition.

0:00–0:12 · Landing Page

"The first thing a contractor or supplier sees is this: a clear promise and a single decision — who are you? We designed this page so that within 10 seconds, anyone in construction knows exactly what Sourced does and whether it's for them. No jargon. No feature list. Just the outcome."

→ Click "I'm a Contractor"
0:12–0:22 · Sign Up → Dashboard

"Sign up is 4 fields. No credit card. 60 seconds. And instead of landing on a blank dashboard, they're immediately guided to post their first requirement — the one action that creates value. We skip the empty state entirely."

→ Complete form. Land on requirement form (guided).
0:22–0:40 · Posting a Requirement

"This is the core action. They tell us what they need — just like texting a supplier. Smart autocomplete means no catalog navigation. The address is pre-filled. Quantity defaults from their last order. Watch what happens when they hit Post…"

→ Tap "Post & Let AI Handle It." Watch the pulse animation. Transition to Live Status.
0:40–0:58 · Bid Comparison — The Wow Moment

"This is the moment clients lean forward. They posted a requirement. The AI contacted 14 suppliers. Now they have 4 ranked bids — and the system has already calculated which one is 12% below market. One tap to accept. No phone calls. No spreadsheets. This is what 24 hours of AI procurement looks like."

→ Tap "Accept This Bid." Watch the confirmation animation.
0:58–1:10 · Order Confirmed + Savings

"And this is what drives retention. Not just 'order confirmed' — but 'you saved $347 on this order.' The number counts up. They see the value. This is the moment they send the app to their project manager."

→ Pause on savings screen. Let the number land.
1:10–1:22 · Close

"The entire experience — from 'I need material' to 'order confirmed, money saved' — is under 2 minutes of the contractor's time. The other 22 hours? That's Sourced working in the background. That's the platform."

12Red Team Analysis

Where This UX Might Fail

⚠
Risk 01 — "AI Recommended" Badge Feels Manipulative
If users don't understand how the AI ranks bids, they may distrust the recommendation or feel herded. Especially cautious contractors who've been burned by "best deal" promises before.
Fix: Add a tiny "Why this recommendation?" link that expands: "Ranked by price, delivery time, and supplier reliability score." Transparency converts skeptics faster than hiding the logic.
⚠
Risk 02 — Countdown Timers Feel Pressuring
"This bid expires in 18h" can trigger reactive rather than considered decisions. Users who feel pressured may abandon instead of accept, or accept and regret.
Fix: Ensure timers are always honest (never shortened). Add: "Not ready? Bids can be refreshed in one tap if they expire." Removes the fear of permanent loss — actually increases acceptance rate by reducing anxiety.
⚠
Risk 03 — Savings Claim Feels Inflated
"14% below market" only works if the market baseline is credible. If users can't verify it, it becomes marketing noise that erodes trust after first use.
Fix: Link "market average" to a methodology page. Show the sample (e.g., "Based on 340 quotes in Rhode Island, Jan–Mar 2026"). Specific beats aspirational. One click to proof.
△
Risk 04 — Onboarding Completion vs. Activation Gap
A user can complete sign-up without ever posting a requirement. The gap between "signed up" and "first value" is the most dangerous drop point and most platforms underinvest here.
Fix: Make posting a requirement part of onboarding itself (Step 4a in this design). Remove the optional path. First-session success means at least one requirement is in the system before they close the app.
△
Risk 05 — Mobile Form Completion Rate
Contractors are on job sites. Typing on mobile while managing a crew is hard. A 4-field form still feels like work if the keyboard behavior is poor.
Fix: Numeric keyboard auto-triggers for quantity fields. Delivery date uses a native date picker, not a typed field. Material autocomplete shows maximum 5 results — never a scroll. Voice input button on material name field.
△
Risk 06 — Complexity Hidden Too Deep (Power Users)
Progressive disclosure risks hiding features that power users would have paid for, making the product feel shallow to the users who can champion it internally.
Fix: Power features unlocked through usage, not time. After 5th requirement: bulk upload appears. After 3rd supplier: preferred list appears. Tie feature reveal to action milestones, not calendar days. Users feel they earned the power, not that it was withheld.

The Core Simplification Principle

Every screen in this framework was designed by asking: "What is the one thing a user needs to do here, and what is everything else?" Everything else either gets removed, collapsed, or deferred. The complexity of construction procurement is real — but it should be invisible to the user. That's the job of the AI agents. The UI's job is to make humans feel capable, not overwhelmed.
